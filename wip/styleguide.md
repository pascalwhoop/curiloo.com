# Curiloo writing style guide

Inspired by Matteo Wong (The Atlantic), Asimov Press, and Quanta Magazine. This is a working guide, not a rulebook. The goal is writing that earns its argument through concrete evidence and narrative momentum rather than assertion.

---

## The core idea

A reader should feel like they are thinking alongside someone smart, not being briefed by a committee. The prose should have a voice — a person who cares about the subject and has thought hard about it — not the neutral, hedged tone of a document that wants to offend no one.

---

## Openings

Start with something concrete: a scene, a paradox, a number that surprises. Not with a claim about what the piece will argue, and not with a definition.

**Avoid:**
> This is a comprehensive assessment of the European Generations Fund proposal.

**Good (Quanta-style — a fact that reframes):**
> Scientists keep detecting new forms of ice. According to simulations, there could be many more left to find.

**Good (Matteo Wong-style — human stakes first, then pull back):**
> What I loved about traditional Google searches is falling into clutter and treasure, all the time, without ever intending to. AI search may close off these avenues.

**Good (Asimov Press-style — tension or revelation):**
> If you listen to a metabolic engineer describe their work, you might hear them hail bacteria as quasi-magical self-replicating nanofactories. Visit their lab and you will see those very nanofactories treated as base sludge — used and then discarded.

The reader should be hooked by the first sentence, not oriented by it.

---

## Sentence rhythm

Alternate between short declarative sentences (emphasis, clarity) and longer subordinate-clause sentences (nuance, connection). Short sentences land. Long ones carry the analysis. Never string three long sentences in a row without a short one to let the reader breathe.

**Avoid:**
> The proposal faces insurmountable political and legal barriers that would prevent implementation, despite addressing a genuine €250+ billion annual pension funding gap, given that EU treaties provide no inheritance tax competence requiring unanimous approval from 27 states.

**Better:**
> The proposal faces long odds. But it identifies the right problem.

The short sentence after the long one is where the meaning lands.

---

## Using data and evidence

A number earns its place by changing how you see something, not by decorating a claim. When you use a statistic, the sentence should do something with it — compare it, contradict an expectation, reveal a scale.

**Avoid:**
> The EU dependency ratio will surge from 36.1% (2022) to 59.1% (2070)—from 2.8 working-age persons per elderly person to just 1.8. Italy's dependency ratio will reach 65.5%.

**Better:**
> By 2050, the EU will have 1.8 working-age adults for every retiree. The systems were built for 4:1.

The second version uses two sentences to do what the first version buries in a parenthetical. The contrast (4:1 vs 1.8:1) is the point; state it as the point.

---

## Tone

Skeptical-but-engaged. Not omniscient, not neutral. A good piece shows the author has taken the opposing view seriously before dismissing it — or, better, has found the place where the opposing view has merit and said so.

Matteo Wong: evidence-first, careful attribution. Asimov Press: personal investment visible, "I felt a deep appreciation for both the speed of life and the cleverness of the human mind." Quanta: wonder and precision in the same sentence, "It's a structure that's been sitting there for 50 years in front of our nose. We just hadn't noticed it."

---

## Structure within sections

- Lead each section with the specific, then widen to significance
- Avoid three-part lists disguised as paragraphs — the pattern of **Bold intro.** One sentence of explanation. **Bold intro.** One sentence of explanation. is a listicle, not prose
- End sections with an implication, not a summary. The final sentence should propel the reader forward or leave them with something to hold onto

**AI summary ending (avoid):**
> In conclusion, the proposal identifies genuine problems but faces significant structural challenges that make implementation difficult.

**Better:**
> The pension arithmetic is not a future problem. The capital exists. The question is whether the structure is worthy of it.

---

## Words and phrases to kill

These are signals that the prose is asserting rather than showing:

| Kill this | Because |
|-----------|---------|
| devastating | Claims emotion rather than earning it |
| stark | Same |
| insurmountable | Same |
| unprecedented | Almost never true; often lazy |
| comprehensive | Means nothing; everything is comprehensive |
| significant / meaningful | Vague intensifiers |
| it is worth noting | Filler; just say the thing |
| importantly | If it's important, the sentence shows it |
| this suggests that | Weakens what should be a clear claim |
| dispositive / orthogonal / plethora | Thesaurus syndrome |
| going forward | Never |
| in order to | Just use "to" |

## Analogies and metaphors

Use them to make the abstract concrete, not to decorate. A metaphor should replace an explanation, not sit next to one.

Asimov Press: "ATP synthase spins around exactly 134 times each second — significantly faster than the propeller on most piston airplanes, and about half the rpm of a Boeing 737 jet engine." The comparison is the explanation.

Quanta: "They can sometimes 'parkour their way to the top of a 6-foot wall' that a human couldn't climb, but the little jumping robots are nowhere near the Mount Everests of math." Two images, one point. The humor is precise.

One strong analogy beats three mediocre ones.

---

## Voice

The reader should sense a human narrator is present. That means:

- Occasional first-person is fine in essays ("I felt," "what surprised me")
- "You" is fine sparingly to bring the reader in
- Taking a position is fine — the best science and policy writing argues, it doesn't merely survey
- Anticipate objections and address them directly: Asimov Press: "Now, I can practically hear your objections through the page!"

What to avoid: the omniscient floating narrator who has no skin in the game and is merely reporting what various studies have found.

---

## A practical test

Read your piece aloud. If you stumble on a sentence, it is probably too long. If a paragraph sounds like a PowerPoint slide, it is probably structured as one. If you cannot identify who the human narrator is, there probably isn't one.

Ask: what is the one sentence in this piece that I most want the reader to remember? If you can identify it, check that it is short, early, and direct. If you cannot identify it, the piece may not have a thesis yet.
