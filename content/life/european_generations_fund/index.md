**The proposal faces insurmountable political and legal barriers that would prevent implementation, despite addressing a genuine €250+ billion annual pension funding gap.** EU treaties provide no inheritance tax competence requiring unanimous approval from 27 states including 8 that have already abolished such taxes. The enhanced cooperation mechanism failed completely for the Financial Transaction Tax after 13 years, and capital flight evidence from Norway (€600 billion departed 2023-24), Sweden (30,000 emigration permits 1965-84), and France (€200 billion since 1988) demonstrates wealthy individuals will relocate when faced with confiscatory taxation. The equity transfer mechanism has no precedent globally—no jurisdiction accepts private company shares for tax payment. While the funding need is real (EU dependency ratio rising from 36% to 59% by 2070), and successful sovereign wealth fund models exist (Norway's $1.9 trillion fund), the proposal's structural flaws make it a cautionary tale in policy design rather than a viable solution.

## The constitutional brick wall

The European Union has **no explicit competence over inheritance taxation** under current treaties, creating an immediate legal impasse. Article 115 TFEU theoretically permits harmonization of laws "directly affecting the internal market" but requires **unanimous Council approval** via special legislative procedure—a requirement that has proven fatal to every ambitious EU tax coordination attempt.

The European Court of Justice has never mandated inheritance tax harmonization. Instead, ECJ rulings focus exclusively on **negative integration**: striking down discriminatory national rules while preserving member state autonomy over tax rates and structure. In *Commission v Spain* (2014), the Court required Spain to extend regional tax benefits to non-residents but did not touch Spain's sovereignty to set inheritance tax rates. Similarly, *Eckelkamp v Belgium* (2008) eliminated residence-based deductions while leaving Belgium's fundamental inheritance tax architecture intact. **The ECJ enforces non-discrimination, not harmonization.**

Treaty amendment to grant explicit EU inheritance tax competence would require ratification by all 27 member states including national parliaments and potentially referenda. Given that **eight EU countries have already abolished inheritance taxes entirely** (Austria in 2008, Sweden in 2004, Estonia, Latvia, Slovakia, Cyprus, Malta, Romania), this path is politically impossible. Sweden's abolition was unanimous—even the Social Democrats, Green Party, and Left Party voted to eliminate the tax after entrepreneurs threatened mass exodus and the Sally Kistner estate went bankrupt from taxes despite being valued at SEK 300 million.

The Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive (ATAD), enacted 2016 with Article 5 covering exit taxation, **explicitly excludes inheritance and gift taxes**. ATAD applies only to corporate taxpayers, business asset transfers, and company tax residence changes. Member states retain complete autonomy over wealth transfer taxes—a deliberate exclusion reflecting political reality.

## Why wealth taxes trigger capital flight

The empirical evidence on behavioral responses to wealth taxation is devastating for this proposal. **Norway's wealth tax increase from 0.9% to 1.1% in 2022** was projected to raise NOK 1.5 billion annually. The actual result: NOK 5.4 billion in lost revenue—a fiscal loss **four times larger than the projected gain**. By 2024, roughly 300 multimillionaires and billionaires had relocated to Switzerland, taking NOK 600+ billion in assets. The tax administration characterized it as an "unprecedented exodus."

France's experience with the Impôt de Solidarité sur la Fortune (ISF) provides similarly stark data. Senator Philippe Marini's 2006 report documented 843 people leaving France annually due to the wealth tax, with a net loss of €2.8 billion in wealth—"around two taxpayers a day." Eric Pichet's authoritative 2007 study calculated **total capital flight of €200 billion** since the tax's 1988 reinstatement, creating an annual fiscal shortfall of €7 billion—**twice what the tax actually yielded in revenue**. Emmanuel Macron abolished the ISF in 2018, explicitly citing evidence that "rich people fled the country instead of paying."

Sweden's wealth tax history, analyzed in NBER Working Paper 32153 (Jakobsen et al., 2024), reveals that a 1 percentage point increase in the wealth tax rate produced a 2% decrease in the stock of wealthy taxpayers through emigration. The study found that **one-third of expatriation events among the top 2% wealthiest were caused by the wealth tax**. When Sweden abolished the tax in 2007, out-migration among top wealth holders dropped 30% immediately. Critically, the researchers found the aggregate economic impact was modest despite dramatic individual departures, suggesting wealthy individuals are highly mobile but their economic contributions may be overstated—a nuanced finding that cuts both ways.

The Swiss JUSO referendum (November 2025) provides the most recent evidence. The proposal for a 50% inheritance tax on estates exceeding CHF 50 million failed with **78.3% voting against and all 26 cantons rejecting it**. Support collapsed from 48% six weeks before the vote to 21.7% at the ballot box. Stefan Legge from the University of St. Gallen noted before the vote that affected individuals "did the paperwork to be sure they are ready to move out if necessary." His research concluded the tax would **reduce net revenue despite the 50% rate**, as those 2,500 affected individuals currently pay CHF 5-6 billion annually in other taxes.

## The enhanced cooperation mechanism already failed

The Financial Transaction Tax provides a devastating precedent for this proposal. In January 2013, the Council authorized enhanced cooperation for 11 member states to implement an FTT. **Thirteen years later, the tax has never been implemented**, and the Commission's October 2025 work programme indicates intention to formally withdraw the proposal.

Estonia withdrew in March 2016 after calculating the tax would generate only €3 million in revenue against €2 million in collection costs—a pattern that should alarm proponents of inheritance tax coordination. The remaining 10 participating states (Belgium, Germany, Greece, Spain, France, Italy, Austria, Portugal, Slovenia, Slovakia) **could not achieve unanimous agreement among themselves** despite all volunteering to participate. Slovenia nearly withdrew in 2014 when the scope narrowed, facing the same cost-benefit calculation as Estonia.

The Council Legal Service's September 2013 opinion declared the FTT proposal **"illegal under international customary law"** for exceeding member states' taxation jurisdiction and incompatible with the EU Treaty. The extraterritoriality problem—taxing transactions involving non-participating states—created insurmountable legal barriers. An inheritance tax on equity stakes in companies with cross-border operations would face identical extraterritoriality challenges.

The FTT failure demonstrates that even the **flexible enhanced cooperation mechanism cannot overcome** fundamental economic barriers in tax matters: capital mobility, free-rider problems, coordination challenges, and the unanimity requirement among participants. Inheritance taxation faces all these obstacles plus the emotional dimension of family wealth transfer and the technical complexity of equity valuation.

## Eastern European opposition is structural

Eight Eastern European states would likely form a **blocking coalition** against inheritance tax coordination, rooted in their post-communist political economy. These countries pursued flat tax revolutions in the 2000s (Slovakia 19%, Czech Republic 15%, Hungary 15%, Estonia 22%) as deliberate tax competition strategy to attract foreign direct investment. Low taxes are their **competitive advantage** in attracting capital within the single market.

The wealth disparity is fundamental. Western European households accumulated wealth over generations; Eastern European households began private wealth accumulation only after 1989-91. These societies are in the **wealth accumulation stage**, not redistribution stage, making inheritance taxation politically toxic. The Czech government explicitly refused to join the FTT, with Prime Minister Petr Nečas calling it "unacceptable" and threatening ECJ challenge.

Enhanced cooperation for inheritance tax would create a **two-tier Europe**: Western states coordinating high wealth taxes, Eastern states operating as internal tax havens within the single market. Free movement of capital and establishment (TFEU Articles 63-64) would enable wealthy individuals to relocate to non-participating states while maintaining business operations in coordinating states—exactly the extraterritoriality problem that killed the FTT.

## The equity transfer mechanism has no precedent

No major jurisdiction allows payment of inheritance tax in private company equity. France's *dation en paiement* permits transferring assets to satisfy tax liability, but **only for cultural property**: works of art, books, historic buildings, forests for conservation. The UK's Acceptance in Lieu scheme offers a 25% premium ("douceur") but again **only for pre-eminent cultural, scientific, or historic objects**. Neither system accepts shares in private family businesses or operating companies.

This absence is not accidental. Equity stakes in private companies present insurmountable practical problems:

**Valuation disputes**: Private company shares have no market price. The gap between tax authority valuations and family valuations would generate endless litigation. Germany's Destatis reports €121.5 billion in assessed inheritance flows for 2023, but economic estimates using the Piketty mortality multiplier method suggest €240-360 billion in actual transfers—a **50-70% gap** reflecting aggressive valuation and extensive exemptions.

**Dilution and capital calls**: The Stille Gesellschaft (silent partnership) structure used by German state investment vehicles typically provides capital without voting rights or governance participation. But when companies face dilution events (new share issuances) or capital calls, does the passive state shareholder participate? Failure to participate in capital calls can trigger severe dilution—in LLC structures, each dollar not contributed during a capital call can cost thousands in fair value due to the gap between book value and market value. The European Generations Fund would face constant decisions about participating in capital calls across thousands of companies.

**Governance and exit problems**: France's Caisse des Dépôts holds stakes in 250+ companies including La Poste (66%), Transdev (34%), and Compagnie des Alpes (40%). These are **strategic, long-term holdings** with professional management and clear investment mandates. A fund receiving random equity stakes from inheritance tax obligations would lack coherent investment strategy, face immense diversification (good) but also exposure to thousands of illiquid private companies (bad), and confront exit problems when stakes need to be liquidated.

**Bankruptcy and restructuring**: In German insolvency law, typical silent partnership claims rank as ordinary unsecured claims; atypical silent partnerships rank subordinated (after all unsecured creditors). Preferred shares rank after all creditors. If the fund holds passive stakes across thousands of private companies, many will inevitably fail. The fund's junior claims would often be worthless, creating a systematic downward bias in returns.

## The pension crisis is real but the solution is wrong

The European Commission's 2024 Ageing Report projects EU27 pension spending will increase modestly from 11.4% of GDP (2022) to 11.8% of GDP (2070)—a seemingly manageable +0.4 percentage point increase. But this masks dramatic country variation and assumes politically unsustainable benefit cuts.

Spain faces a **+3.6 percentage point increase** to 16.7% of GDP by 2070. Luxembourg: +8.3 percentage points to 17.5%. Malta: +4.4 percentage points. Germany: +1.2 percentage points to 11.4% of GDP. The EU dependency ratio will surge from 36.1% (2022) to 59.1% (2070)—from 2.8 working-age persons per elderly person to just 1.8. Italy's dependency ratio will reach 65.5%, Portugal's 67.8%, Lithuania's 72.4%.

The baseline projection assumes the **benefit ratio declines from 43% to 36%**—a 7 percentage point reduction in the average pension relative to average wage. This represents massive real benefit cuts that may prove "politically untenable" (ECB analysis). If this benefit ratio decline is reversed through political pressure, pension spending would rise far more than projected.

But the European Generations Fund does not solve this problem efficiently. The fund would take decades to scale, face capital flight that reduces net revenue, and create governance nightmares. **Direct approaches work better**: raising retirement ages (23 OECD countries already doing this, with normal retirement age rising from 64.4 to 66.3 years), means-testing benefits, automating benefit adjustments to life expectancy (13 eurozone countries have such mechanisms), or consumption taxation to fund the transition.

## Supporting evidence for sovereign wealth funds exists, but context matters

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global demonstrates that sovereign wealth funds can work spectacularly. Starting from NOK 734,000 in 1977, the fund has grown to **$1.9 trillion (NOK 19,742 billion)** as of June 2025—over $340,000 per Norwegian citizen. The fund achieved **6.34% average annual returns since 1998** (4.1% real return after inflation), owns 1.5% of all listed companies worldwide, and maintains **perfect political independence** through a three-tier governance structure.

The fund's **fiscal rule** permits government withdrawal of up to 3% of fund value annually (reduced from 4% in 2017 based on expert commission recommendations), with actual withdrawals typically below 3% to maintain buffers. This rule has held for two decades, demonstrating that constitutional safeguards and transparent governance can prevent political raids on sovereign wealth.

New Zealand's Superannuation Fund proves that **non-resource-based capitalization works**. Funded entirely from general taxation and budget surpluses (not oil revenues), the fund has grown to NZ$70+ billion with **10.26% annualized returns since inception in 2003**. Contributions were suspended 2009-2017 during austerity but resumed, showing vulnerability to political cycles but also resilience.

Singapore's dual structure (GIC for passive reserve management, Temasek for active ownership) demonstrates sophisticated state equity management. GIC achieves **5.7% nominal returns (3.8% real) over 20 years** while maintaining strict confidentiality about size (estimated $744 billion to $1.8 trillion). Temasek achieved **14% compounded returns over 50 years** through active ownership of operating companies, proving that state entities can achieve strong commercial returns.

But these successes operate in fundamentally different contexts:

**Norway has resource rents**—pure economic surplus from non-reproducible natural assets. All petroleum surplus flows automatically to the fund. An inheritance tax generates no comparable surplus; it's a transfer from citizens that triggers behavioral responses (emigration, restructuring, valuation manipulation).

**Singapore and New Zealand have political consensus** and small, cohesive populations (5.2 million and 5 million respectively). The EU27 has 450 million people, 27 different political systems, languages, legal traditions, and fiscal cultures. Norway has one government; the EU requires coordination across 27 national governments plus EU institutions.

**Alaska's dividend model** ($1,702 per person in 2024) demonstrates that direct citizen benefits build political support, but also creates volatility—the 2025 dividend dropped to $1,000 as the Legislature sets amounts via appropriation rather than automatic formula. Research shows the Alaska dividend reduced poverty 20-40% without reducing work incentives, but it works because of massive resource rents relative to a tiny population (600,000 eligible Alaskans).

## What the academic proposals actually say

Thomas Piketty's research documents that **inheritance flows have risen to 15% of national income in France** (2010), up from 5% in 1950—a threefold increase driven by rising wealth-to-income ratios (from 200-300% of GDP in the 1970s to 400-600% today) and aging populations. Germany's inheritance flows exceed 10% of national income (€220 billion in 2009, €121.5 billion assessed in 2023). Italy reached 15% of national income (€250 billion annually) in 2016, doubled from 7-8% in the mid-1990s.

This return of inherited wealth represents a **fundamental shift from meritocracy to inherited advantage**. The French CAE "Rethinking Inheritance" report (December 2021) found that the top 0.1% inherit an average €13 million net of taxes, while the median inheritance is below €70,000. The ratio is **1:180**—compared to labor income's 1:10 ratio between top 0.1% and median. Inherited wealth now represents 60% of total wealth in France, versus 35% in the early 1970s.

But the CAE's reform proposals focus on **lifetime-based taxation** (taxing cumulative lifetime inheritance receipts rather than each transfer separately), **eliminating exemptions** (the Dutreil Pact costs €2-3 billion, life insurance exemptions €4-5 billion), and **universal capital endowments** (€10,000 to all young adults) funded by closing loopholes. Critically, they propose reforms that could raise **€19 billion additional revenue** in France alone by capping business exemptions at 50% (versus current 75%), integrating life insurance, and taxing unrealized capital gains at resale.

The IPPR Citizens' Wealth Fund proposal (UK, 2018) suggested £10,000 to all 25-year-olds starting 2030, requiring a fund of £186 billion by 2029/30. Capitalization would come from asset sales, capital transfers, new revenue streams, and borrowing at low interest rates. This is a **closed national system** within a sovereign state, not a cross-border coordination mechanism—a crucial difference from the EU proposal.

Anthony Atkinson's "inheritance for all" (2015) proposed £5,000-£10,000 capital endowments at age 18, funded by estate tax reform with progressive rates up to 65% on larger estates and a lifetime capital receipts tax. DIW Berlin's €20,000 universal inheritance proposal (2021) calculated annual cost of €22.6 billion for 750,000 young adults per cohort, funded by restricting business exemptions (capped at €1-2 million versus current unlimited 75% exemption) and a wealth tax on net worth exceeding €20 million (affecting just 21,000 adults or 0.03% of population).

**These are all national proposals** within functioning fiscal states with sovereign taxing power, not EU coordination mechanisms requiring unanimous approval from 27 states. The gap between academic proposals and political feasibility is vast.

## Hard data reveals the scale and the challenge

Total household wealth in the euro area (20 countries) reached **€61 trillion in Q4 2024** according to ECB Distributional Wealth Accounts. The **top 10% hold 57.3% of this wealth**; the top 5% hold approximately 44%; the bottom 50% hold just 2%. EU27 GDP in 2024 was €17.9-18.0 trillion.

The €5 million threshold is critical but **no official EU statistics publish household counts by absolute wealth thresholds**. ECB data provides deciles and top percentile shares but not counts above specific euro amounts. Global wealth reports (UBS/Credit Suisse) estimate that individuals with over €5 million in assets globally hold approximately €119 trillion, with Europe hosting roughly 25% of global top 1% wealth holders—suggesting several hundred thousand EU households above the threshold, but precise data remains elusive.

Current inheritance tax revenues across the EU are remarkably low: **Belgium 1.46% of GDP** (highest), France 1.36%, UK 0.71%, Spain 0.58%, Germany 0.52%, Italy 0.10%. The OECD average for countries with inheritance tax is just **0.5% of total tax revenue**. Eight EU countries collect zero because they've abolished the tax entirely.

The **40% inheritance flow gap** documented in the French CAE report—the difference between declared (taxed) transfers and actual economic flows—reveals massive base erosion. Germany's gap is similar: €121.5 billion assessed by tax authorities versus €240-360 billion estimated economic transfers. Business exemptions, life insurance loopholes, property dismemberment structures, and undervaluation create a "Swiss cheese" tax base.

The pension funding gap that motivates this proposal is substantial but concentrated. **Spain's pension spending rises 3.6 percentage points of GDP** by 2070 (from 13.1% to 16.7%), representing roughly €65 billion annually at 2070 GDP levels assuming modest growth. Germany's increase of 1.2 percentage points translates to approximately €50-60 billion annually. These are large numbers, but inheritance tax coordination faces **net revenue uncertainty** due to behavioral responses.

## Mechanism design encounters insurmountable barriers

The equity stake transfer mechanism fails at every level of analysis. **Silent partnership (Stille Gesellschaft) structures** used by German MBGs provide subordinated capital without voting rights or external visibility, but require bilateral agreements between investor and company. Scaling this to thousands of forced transfers from inheritance tax obligations creates:

**Valuation warfare**: Every estate would dispute the assessed value of private company shares. Germany's family business lobby is already powerful enough to secure unlimited 75% Dutreil exemptions for business assets. Forcing equity transfer at tax authority valuations would generate immediate legislative backlash and endless litigation.

**Portfolio incoherence**: A well-designed sovereign wealth fund like Norway's GPFG or Singapore's GIC invests globally across liquid securities to achieve diversification and minimize home bias. The European Generations Fund would receive **random equity stakes in illiquid private companies** concentrated in sectors and geographies determined by inheritance patterns, not investment strategy. This violates basic portfolio theory.

**Governance impossibility**: Germany's Caisse des Dépôts model works because it makes **strategic, long-term investments** with professional due diligence and active governance (even when holding minority stakes). Passive receipt of equity from tax obligations provides no opportunity for due diligence, negotiation of governance rights, or strategic selection. The fund would be a forced buyer of whatever assets the inheritance tax captures.

**Exit problems**: Preferred non-voting shares and silent partnerships typically require **redemption mechanisms** (put options, conversion rights, drag-along rights). But inheritance tax obligations cannot negotiate these rights—they're imposed by law. Without negotiated exit rights, the fund would hold illiquid stakes indefinitely, unable to rebalance or exit failed investments.

**Dilution cascades**: The Texas law example reveals that pre-emptive rights **exempt** shares issued for employee compensation, conversions, options, and non-monetary consideration. In practice, private companies conducting capital raises could dilute the passive state shareholder to insignificance while friendly investors maintain their percentage through participation. The fund would need legal protections, but imposing such protections on private companies via tax law would trigger immediate political opposition.

**The fundamental problem**: This mechanism attempts to solve a **tax collection problem** (how to extract revenue) through **forced equity investment** (acquiring random stakes in private companies). These are orthogonal objectives. Norway solves the investment problem by taking cash oil revenues and investing globally. The EU proposal conflates collection and investment, creating a Frankenstein structure that fails at both.

## Alternative approaches deserve consideration

If the goal is addressing Europe's pension funding gap, several approaches have better feasibility:

**Consumption taxation**: The CEPR 2023 study noted that France and Italy have "least fiscal leeway on wages to counter pension decline" because labor taxation already approaches optimal levels. Consumption taxes (VAT increases) face less behavioral response than wealth taxes and are already harmonized across the EU. A 1 percentage point VAT increase across the EU would raise approximately €18-20 billion annually.

**Parametric pension reform**: Automatically adjusting retirement ages to life expectancy (13 eurozone countries have such mechanisms) and means-testing benefits for high earners could close much of the funding gap without new revenue. Germany's retirement age is already rising to 67; further increases tied to life expectancy gains would reduce spending 1-2 percentage points of GDP.

**Voluntary coordination on inheritance tax information exchange**: The EU's Directive on Administrative Cooperation (DAC) already provides automatic exchange of financial information. Extending this to **comprehensive inheritance and gift reporting** would reduce the 40-50% base erosion from non-reporting without requiring harmonized rates. Countries could maintain sovereignty while limiting avoidance.

**OECD-led global minimum inheritance tax**: The 2022 Pillar Two global minimum corporate tax (15%) succeeded precisely because it had **US/OECD backing** creating external pressure. An OECD initiative on minimum inheritance taxation for estates above €10 million (or similar threshold) would avoid the EU unanimity trap. The G20 framework would make coordination more credible.

**EU-level transparency on beneficial ownership**: The bigger problem than rate coordination is **visibility**. Strengthening beneficial ownership registers, closing trust and foundation loopholes, and requiring third-party reporting of large inheritances (following OECD 2021 recommendations) would raise revenue without harmonizing rates.

**National sovereign wealth funds capitalized from general revenues**: Following New Zealand's model, individual countries with fiscal capacity could establish sovereign wealth funds capitalized from budget surpluses or borrowing (at current low rates) without cross-border coordination. Germany, Netherlands, Denmark, and Sweden have fiscal space for this approach.

## Where the proposal has merit

Despite fundamental flaws, the proposal identifies **three genuine insights**:

First, **inherited wealth concentration is rising dramatically** and threatens meritocratic principles. The data from Piketty, the French CAE, and national statistics offices is unambiguous: inheritance flows have tripled as share of national income since the 1950s, wealth-to-income ratios have doubled, and the returns to inherited capital increasingly exceed returns to labor. This is a real problem requiring policy response.

Second, **pension funding gaps are structural, not cyclical**. The EU dependency ratio increase from 36% to 59% by 2070 represents a fundamental demographic shift, not a temporary challenge. Current pension promises are likely unsustainable without either benefit cuts (the baseline assumes 7 percentage point benefit ratio decline) or new revenue sources. The funding need is real.

Third, **sovereign wealth funds can work** when properly designed. Norway, Singapore, New Zealand, and Alaska demonstrate that patient capital managed professionally can achieve strong returns while serving public purposes. The governance innovations—three-tier separation (Norway), independent Crown entity (New Zealand), constitutional protection (Alaska)—provide models worth studying.

But recognizing these problems does not make this particular solution viable. **Good diagnosis does not guarantee effective treatment.**

## The verdict

This proposal fails on three critical dimensions: **legal feasibility** (no EU competence, unanimity required, 8 countries already abolished inheritance taxes), **economic feasibility** (overwhelming capital flight evidence, no precedent for equity transfer mechanisms, governance problems insurmountable), and **political feasibility** (enhanced cooperation failed for FTT after 13 years, Eastern European blocking coalition likely, two-tier Europe risk).

The Swiss referendum result—78% rejection with all 26 cantons opposed—demonstrates that even **national proposals** face overwhelming opposition when rates approach confiscatory levels. An EU-wide proposal requiring coordination across 27 states, transfer of tax sovereignty, and acceptance of untested equity mechanisms would face exponentially greater obstacles.

The **capital flight evidence is dispositive**: Norway lost €600 billion in assets and NOK 5.4 billion in net revenue from a modest wealth tax increase; France lost €200 billion over 30 years from the ISF; Sweden experienced 30,000 emigration permits for wealth transfers over two decades; Estonia withdrew from FTT enhanced cooperation when costs exceeded benefits. Mobile capital responds to taxation by relocating, and the €5 million threshold captures precisely the **most mobile wealth holders**.

The equity transfer mechanism remains the proposal's **Achilles heel**. No working example exists globally. The practical problems—valuation disputes, portfolio incoherence, governance impossibility, dilution risks, exit barriers—are not implementation details but fundamental design flaws. Forcing a sovereign wealth fund to accept random equity stakes from tax obligations violates every principle of sound portfolio management.

If the goal is genuinely addressing pension sustainability and wealth inequality, the path forward involves: automatic pension reforms (retirement age adjustments, means-testing), consumption tax increases to fund the transition, national sovereign wealth funds capitalized from general revenues (following New Zealand), enhanced information exchange and beneficial ownership transparency to reduce tax base erosion, and potentially OECD-led coordination on minimum standards rather than EU-only harmonization.

The European Generations Fund represents **sophisticated policy thinking applied to genuine problems**—but its structural contradictions, political infeasibility, and lack of working precedents make it a thought experiment rather than an actionable proposal. Sometimes the most important contribution of ambitious policy proposals is clarifying the **boundaries of the politically possible** and directing attention toward less elegant but more viable alternatives.
