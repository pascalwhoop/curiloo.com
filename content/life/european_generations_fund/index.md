*Bridging Europe's pension gap through inherited wealth*

By 2050, the EU will have 1.8 working-age adults for every retiree — down from 2.8 today. Current pension systems were built for a ratio of 4:1. The EU already spends nearly €2 trillion per year on pensions, and that figure is rising. Official projections assume steep benefit cuts to contain costs; without those cuts, the additional funding gap runs to hundreds of billions per year. Europe needs a new source of capital, and it is already sitting in the balance sheets of its wealthiest households.

## The asset

European households hold €61 trillion in net wealth. The largest intergenerational transfer in modern history is underway as baby boomers age: inheritance flows now exceed 10–15% of national income across France, Germany, and Italy — roughly **€1.5–2.4 trillion per year** across the EU. Most of it passes lightly taxed or untaxed. Eight member states have no inheritance tax at all.

## The mechanism

Heirs of estates exceeding €5 million transfer a percentage — say 20–30% of the amount above the threshold — not as cash, but as a **passive equity stake** to the European Generations Fund. No forced sales. No liquidation events. The family retains operational control. The fund holds silently, like a minority investor with no governance rights, collecting its share of dividends and value appreciation.

For publicly traded assets, the transfer is trivial. For private companies and real estate, the fund accepts a silent partnership stake or equivalent instrument — structures that already exist in European corporate law.

The €5 million floor means this touches only the top 1–2% of estates. The median household, the family home, the small savings account — none of it is affected.

## The alignment

An EU-wide exit tax ensures the mechanism holds. Anyone who emigrates from the EU with assets above €5 million crystallises the same percentage — but **in cash, immediately**. Staying is always the better deal. The equity transfer at death is the gentle option; the exit tax is the alternative. This inverts the usual capital flight dynamic: instead of wealthy families leaving to avoid tax, the structure makes staying strictly preferable.

## The math

Conservatively, taxable inheritance flows from estates above €5M amount to €375–700 billion per year across the EU. At a 25% equity transfer rate:

| Year | Cumulative inflows | Fund value (5% return) | Annual income |
|------|-------------------|------------------------|---------------|
| 5    | €500–750B         | €600–900B              | €30–45B       |
| 10   | €1.0–1.5T         | €1.5–2.2T              | €75–110B      |
| 20   | €2.0–3.0T         | €4–7T                  | €200–350B     |

For context: the EU currently spends €1.97 trillion per year on pensions. The Commission's baseline projections assume the average pension benefit will fall from 43% to 36% of the average wage by 2070 — a politically explosive cut. If benefits are instead maintained at current levels, the additional cost runs to 1–2 percentage points of GDP, or roughly **€180–360 billion per year** by mid-century. At the twenty-year mark, the fund's annual income of €200–350 billion begins to match that gap.

Norway built a $1.9 trillion fund from oil revenues alone, serving 5.5 million people. An inheritance-funded EU vehicle, serving 450 million, would join it among the largest sovereign wealth funds in the world within a decade — and its inflows would dwarf those of any resource-based fund, because Europe's inheritance flows are structurally larger and growing.

## Why it works

**Equity, not cash.** Traditional inheritance taxes force asset sales to pay the bill — destroying family businesses, triggering fire sales, creating political opposition. The equity transfer solves this entirely. Nothing is sold. Nothing changes hands operationally. The fund simply becomes a silent co-owner of wealth that society helped create.

**EU-wide, not national.** Single-country inheritance taxes fail because wealthy families relocate one border over. An EU-wide mechanism with an exit tax means leaving the entire single market — a far harder decision. Enhanced cooperation among willing states (minimum nine) could launch the fund; others join as the model proves itself.

**Pensions, not redistribution.** Revenue flows to a sovereign wealth fund with a clear mandate: supplement pension systems as dependency ratios deteriorate. This is not about taking from the rich to give to the poor. It's about ensuring the generation that built Europe's wealth also funds the retirement of those who sustained it. The fund operates like Norway's — transparent, professionally managed, constitutionally ring-fenced.

The timing is structural: the baby boomer wealth transfer peaks in the 2030s–2040s, precisely when the pension burden hits hardest. The demographic problem contains its own solution.

## The invitation

This concept needs rigorous economic modelling, legal analysis, and stress-testing by people who build fiscal policy for a living. What it offers is a **novel mechanism** — the equity transfer — that resolves the three objections that have killed every previous inheritance tax proposal: liquidity (no forced sales), capital flight (exit tax alignment), and political framing (pensions, not redistribution).

Europe's pension arithmetic is not a future problem. It is a present one. The capital to address it exists. The question is whether we design a structure worthy of deploying it.
